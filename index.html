Robot Policy Learning under Physics Priors

RSS 2025 Workshop

2025

Overview

The field of robotics is constantly evolving, requiring robots to operate effectively in complex and unstructured environments. A critical aspect of this capability is the ability of robots to learn policies for tasks such as motion planning, manipulation, and control. For robots to operate in unstructured environments, they need policy learning methods that demonstrate three key characteristics: (1) Training Efficiency: These methods should have low computational complexity during training to enable easy deployment in unknown domains. (2) Inference Efficiency: They must be able to provide solutions to motion planning, manipulation, or control problems in nearly real-time. (3) Adaptability: The methods should be adaptable to complex and high-dimensional robot spaces. While recent advancements have led to both model-based and model-free policy learning approaches, many of these methods fall short in one or more of the aforementioned areas. For instance, Reinforcement Learning and Imitation Learning approaches tend to be inefficient in terms of training, while traditional search or sampling-based methods for policy learning often struggle with inference efficiency.

This workshop aims to explore how integrating physics priors—structured knowledge about the physical world or Partial Differential Equations—into robot policy learning can enhance training efficiency, inference efficiency, and adaptability to unstructured complex environments. It will serve as an interdisciplinary and collaborative platform for researchers in robot motion planning, control, physics-informed machine learning, and computational mathematics to exchange insights and knowledge. The goal is to discuss pathways toward developing physics-driven policy learning methods that support scalable and efficient robot learning in practical scenarios.
